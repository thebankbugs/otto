Technical Analysis Basics

Deconstructing visual chart variations, mastering horizontal support and resistance zone mapping, and tracking entry data using programmatic technical indicators.

1. Chart Types & Reading Japanese Candlesticks

Technical analysis relies on the core principle that all public fundamental news and market sentiments are instantly factored directly into raw price actions. While standard line charts only trace basic daily closing metrics, professional retail platforms utilize **Japanese Candlestick Charts** to extract deep execution insights.

A single candlestick plots price fluctuations across a specific timeframe (e.g., 1 Hour or 4 Hours) using 4 distinct data points: Open, High, Low, and Close (OHLC). The structure is broken down into two components:

  • • The Candle Body: The solid filled block that marks the exact distance between the opening valuation and the closing valuation within that session window.
  • • The Wicks / Shadows: The thin lines stretching above and below the main body. These track the absolute extreme high and low price points touched during the timeframe before institutional orders pulled back.

2. Mapping Market Structure: Support & Resistance

Market prices fluctuate through recurring psychological patterns known as trends. To isolate profitable opportunities, traders map horizontal areas where order flows historically reverse:

  • • Support (The Floor): A localized low price zone where buying interest historically outweighs selling pressure. Institutional orders step in to absorb sell positions, causing the chart to bounce upward.
  • • Resistance (The Ceiling): A localized peak price zone where massive sell blocks outweigh buying enthusiasm. Supply overloads demand, forcing the chart back down.
  • • Role Reversal: Once a strong resistance level is broken by a massive wave of buying volume, it structurally flips into a new support floor when the market returns to retest that area.

3. Programmatic Trading Indicators & Technical Overlays

Technical indicators are mathematical calculation overlays that process historical price data and volumes to smooth out noise and highlight specific probabilities. Beginners should start with two core baseline tools:

A. Moving Averages (MA / EMA)

Moving Averages continuously compute the average closing price across a set number of historical candles (e.g., 50 or 200 sessions). When the price stays cleanly above the moving average line, it signals a strong macroeconomic upward trend. When price acts beneath it, a structural downward cycle is active.

B. The Relative Strength Index (RSI)

The RSI is a momentum tracking oscillator bound between a scale of 0 and 100. It measures the absolute speed and change of price adjustments. Traditional system readings above 70 indicate an overextended 'Overbought' condition ripe for a downward pullback, while metrics beneath 30 point to an 'Oversold' zone where buy orders may form.

C. The Confirmation Fallacy

Indicators should only ever be utilized as supplementary data tools to confirm your raw price action setups. Layering your trading screen with 5 or 6 messy indicator panels creates a structural barrier called 'Analysis Paralysis', where conflicting system metrics ultimately stop you from making clean execution decisions.
